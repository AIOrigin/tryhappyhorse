Cinematic quality is what separates AI video that looks like a demo from AI video that looks like it belongs in a real production. The difference usually comes down to prompting: specific camera direction, intentional lighting, and clear mood language.

This collection gives you 15+ ready-to-use cinematic prompts organized by genre, plus the underlying principles so you can write your own.

## The cinematic prompt formula

Every effective cinematic prompt follows this structure:

1. **Subject** -- Who or what is on screen
2. **Action** -- What is happening
3. **Environment** -- Where the scene takes place
4. **Camera movement** -- How the camera moves (dolly, pan, tilt, orbit, static)
5. **Camera angle** -- Shot type (close-up, wide, medium, over-the-shoulder, low angle)
6. **Lighting** -- Light source, direction, quality (soft, hard, rim, motivated)
7. **Mood and grade** -- Emotional tone and color palette
8. **Duration** -- How long the clip should be

The more of these elements you specify, the more cinematic and controlled the output.

## Camera movement reference guide

To modify these prompts for your own scenes, here are the most effective camera movements for cinematic AI video:

- **Dolly in/push in** -- Increases intimacy, draws viewer closer to subject. Use for emotional moments.
- **Dolly out/pull back** -- Creates distance, reveals context. Use for establishing scope or creating loneliness.
- **Orbit/arc** -- Circles the subject. Creates dynamic energy and dimensionality. Use for hero moments.
- **Tracking/lateral** -- Moves alongside action. Creates forward momentum. Use for movement and pursuit.
- **Crane up/down** -- Vertical movement. Use for reveals (crane up to show landscape) or intimacy (crane down to character).
- **Static** -- No movement. Creates tension, formality, or observational distance. Underrated in AI generation.
- **Handheld drift** -- Subtle, imperfect movement. Creates documentary realism and intimacy.

## Lighting reference guide

Lighting language that AI models tend to respond to well:

- **Key light** -- Primary light source. Specify direction: "key light from upper left"
- **Rim light / backlight** -- Light from behind the subject creating an edge glow. "Strong rim light separating subject from background"
- **Practical light** -- Light from objects in the scene (lamps, screens, candles). "Warm practical light from a desk lamp"
- **Motivated light** -- Light that has a visible or implied source. "Sunlight streaming through venetian blinds"
- **High key** -- Bright, even lighting. "High key studio lighting, minimal shadows"
- **Low key** -- Dark, contrasty lighting. "Low key lighting, deep shadows, single hard light source"
- **Golden hour** -- Warm directional sunlight. "Golden hour side lighting, long shadows, warm amber tones"
